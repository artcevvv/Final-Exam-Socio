Final Exam

Key points through the course of Sociology

Weeks 1-5

Research Methods & Core Concepts

  • Qualitative & Quantitative Research:
    • Qualitative: Focuses on understanding meaning, using methods like interviews, observations, and content analysis.
    • Quantitative: Emphasizes numerical data and statistical analysis, using surveys and experiments.
  • Pilot Study: A small-scale preliminary study conducted to evaluate feasibility, time, cost, adverse events, and improve upon the study design prior to performance of a full-scale research project.
  • Probability Sampling: Every member of the population has a known chance of being selected (e.g., random sampling).
  • Non-Probability Sampling: Selection is not random, often based on convenience or specific criteria (e.g., snowball sampling).
  • Sociological Imagination (C. Wright Mills): The ability to connect personal experiences to broader social forces.
  • Self-Fulfilling Prophecy: A prediction that causes itself to become true.
  • Pygmalion Effect: High expectations lead to increased performance.
  • Matthew Effect: The rich get richer, and the poor get poorer.
  • Anomie (Durkheim): A state of normlessness, leading to social instability.
  • Iron Cage (Weber): The increasing rationalization of social life traps individuals.
  • Overton Window: The range of ideas tolerated in public discourse.
  • Echo Chamber Effect: Reinforcement of existing beliefs within a closed system.
  • Symbolic Interactionism (Mead): Focuses on how individuals create meaning through social interaction.
  • Structural Functionalism (Durkheim): Views society as a system with interdependent parts that function to maintain stability.
  • Conflict Approach (Marx): Emphasizes power struggles and inequality in society.
  • Auguste Comte: Father of sociology, positivism.
  • Emile Durkheim: Social facts, social solidarity, anomie.
  • Max Weber: Verstehen, bureaucracy, rationalization.
  • Karl Marx: Class conflict, capitalism, alienation.
  • George Herbert Mead: Development of self through social interaction.
  • C. Wright Mills: Sociological imagination, power elite.
  • Systems of Stratification: Hierarchical ranking of individuals or groups in society.
  • Vertical & Horizontal Social Mobility:
    • Vertical: Movement up or down the social hierarchy.
    • Horizontal: Movement within the same social level.
  • Dimensions of Stratification: Class, status, and power (Weber).
  • Law of Three Stages (Comte): Theological, metaphysical, and positive stages of societal development.
  • Social Stratification: The hierarchical arrangement of individuals and groups in society.
  • Apartheid: A system of racial segregation and discrimination.

Weeks 6-9

Week 6: Socialization & Identity

  • Socialization: The process of learning norms, values, and behaviors of society.
  • Agents of Socialization:
    • Primary: Family.
    • Secondary: School, peers, media, workplace.
  • Identity: Sense of self, shaped by social interactions.
  • Gender: Socially constructed roles and expectations associated with sex.
    • Masculinity & Femininity: Cultural definitions of appropriate behavior for each gender.
  • Theoretical Approaches to Socialization: Functionalist, conflict, symbolic interactionist.
  • Looking-Glass Self (Cooley): We see ourselves as we believe others see us.
  • Mead’s Role-Taking Theory: Development of self through taking the role of the other.
  • Freud’s Theory: Id (pleasure), ego (reality), superego (morality).
  • Piaget’s Cognitive Development Theory: Stages of cognitive development (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational).

Week 7: Deviance & Crime

  • Deviance: Behavior that violates social norms.
  • Crime: Violation of legal codes.
  • Types of Crime:
    • Violent, nonviolent, corporate, street, victimless.
  • Broken Windows Theory: Minor disorder leads to more serious crime.
  • Crime Hot Spots: Areas with high crime rates.
  • Theoretical Approaches to Crime & Deviance: Strain theory, labeling theory, control theory.
  • Social Control: Mechanisms to regulate behavior.
    • Formal (laws) and informal (social norms).
  • Anomie: Normlessness, contributing to deviance.

Week 8: Family & Marriage

  • Family: Social institution that unites individuals in cooperative groups.
  • Marriage: Socially recognized union.
  • Types of Family:
    • Nuclear, extended, exogamy, endogamy, monogamy, polygamy (polygyny, polyandry), patrilineal, matrilineal, patrilocal, matrilocal, neolocal.
  • Theoretical Perspectives on the Family: Structural functionalism, conflict theory, symbolic interactionism.
  • Inequality Within the Family: Power dynamics, division of labor.
  • Divorce & Separation: Legal and social dissolution of marriage.
  • Second Shift: Women's housework after paid employment.

Week 9: Urbanization, Globalization & Migration

  • Urbanization: Growth of cities.
  • Secularization: Decline of religious influence.
  • Migration: Movement of people from one place to another.
    • Types of Migration: Internal, international, voluntary, forced.
  • Push & Pull Factors of Migration: Reasons for leaving or arriving in a location.
  • Consumer Culture: Society where consumption is central.
  • Globalization: Increasing interconnectedness of societies.
  • Melting Pot: Blending of cultures.
  • Assimilation: Adoption of the dominant culture.
  • Rule Makers, Rule Breakers (Gelfand): Tight and loose cultures, and how they effect society.
  • The Strength of Weak Ties (Granovetter): The importance of acquaintances in social networks.

Key Readings

Rule Makers, Rule Breakers by Gelfand

  • Core Concept:
    • The book explores the concept of "tight" and "loose" cultures. These terms refer to how strictly a society adheres to social norms.
    • "Tight" cultures have strong social norms and little tolerance for deviance.
    • "Loose" cultures have weaker social norms and greater tolerance for deviance.
  • Key Ideas:
    • Gelfand's research shows that this tight-loose dimension influences a wide range of behaviors, from everyday interactions to national policies.
    • She argues that the degree of "tightness" or "looseness" in a culture is often a response to ecological and historical threats. For example, cultures that have experienced frequent natural disasters or invasions tend to be tighter.
    • The book delves into how these cultural differences manifest in various aspects of life, including:
      • Politics
      • Business
      • Family dynamics
      • Individual behavior
  • Impact:
    • The book provides a framework for understanding cultural differences and why people from different backgrounds may behave in different ways.
    • It gives insight into why some countries, states, and even companies have very different social norms.
  • Essentially:
    • The book helps to explain why different groups of people have such varried behaviors, and how those behaviors are formed.

The Strength of Weak Ties by Granovetter

Core Idea:

  • The central argument is that "weak ties"—casual acquaintances rather than close friends—are often more valuable for accessing information and opportunities outside of our immediate social circles.

Key Points:

  • Strong Ties vs. Weak Ties:
    • Strong ties are close relationships, such as family and close friends. These connections tend to share similar information and social circles.
    • Weak ties are acquaintances, distant relatives, or people we know casually. These connections are more likely to have access to different information and networks than our strong ties.
  • Information Flow:
    • Granovetter argues that weak ties act as "bridges" between different social groups. They provide access to novel information, ideas, and opportunities that we wouldn't encounter within our close-knit circles.
    • This is particularly relevant for things like job searches, where weak ties can provide leads to openings that aren't publicly advertised.
  • Implications:
    • The theory has significant implications for understanding how information spreads through social networks, how people find jobs, and how communities are organized.
    • It highlights the importance of maintaining a diverse network of connections, including those with whom we have only casual relationships.
  • Modern Relevance:
    • Even with the rise of social media, Granovetter's theory remains relevant. Platforms like LinkedIn demonstrate the value of weak ties in professional networking.
    • Recent studies, including those using data from LinkedIn, have provided further empirical support for the "strength of weak ties" concept.
